7 Business English Phrases That Will Make You Sound Like a Native

James Carter
April 28, 2026 6 min read
Home / Blog / 7 Business English Phrases

If you've been studying English for years but still feel like something's missing in professional conversations — it might not be your grammar. It's often the phrases. Native speakers rely on a set of idiomatic, context-specific expressions that signal fluency far more than perfect syntax.

Here are seven Business English phrases that will immediately elevate your professional communication.

1. "Let me circle back on that."

Used in meetings when you want to return to a topic later. It shows that you're organised, attentive and not dismissing the point — just prioritising the agenda. Example: "Good point. Let me circle back on that after we've reviewed the budget."

2. "I'd like to table this for now."

In American English, this means to postpone a discussion. (Caution: in British English it means the opposite — to bring something up for discussion.) In most international business contexts, the American usage has become dominant. Example: "I think we should table the pricing discussion until we have more data."

"The right phrase at the right moment isn't about sounding smart — it's about making sure the other person understands exactly what you mean, instantly." — James Carter, LingoUp English Instructor

3. "That's a fair point."

One of the most useful phrases in any negotiation or discussion. It acknowledges the other person's argument without necessarily agreeing with it. It buys you time and builds goodwill. Example: "That's a fair point. Let me think about how we could address that concern."

4. "To be on the same page..."

Used to confirm that everyone shares the same understanding before moving forward. Essential in multicultural teams where assumptions are dangerous. Example: "Just to make sure we're on the same page — the deadline is the 15th, correct?"

5. "I'll take that on board."

A polite, professional way of saying you've heard feedback and will consider it. Common in British-influenced professional environments and widely understood globally. Example: "Thanks for the feedback on the report. I'll take that on board for the next version."

6. "Going forward..."

A transitional phrase that sets expectations for future behaviour or processes. It's clean, forward-looking and avoids assigning blame for past issues. Example: "Going forward, all approvals will need to come through the project manager."

7. "Let's touch base after the call."

Suggests a follow-up conversation. Informal enough for colleagues, professional enough for clients. Example: "I think there's more to discuss here — let's touch base after the call this afternoon."
